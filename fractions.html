Fractions

A fraction represents a part of a whole. It consists of two numbers: the numerator (top number) and the denominator (bottom number).

For example, in 3/4:

  • The numerator is 3
  • The denominator is 4

This means we have 3 parts out of 4 equal parts.

Importance of Fractions

Fractions are widely used in:

  • Mathematics (algebra, calculus)
  • Finance (interest rates, ratios)
  • Measurements (inches, cups)
  • Everyday calculations (recipes, discounts)

Types of Fractions

Proper Fractions

Numerator is smaller than denominator.

Example: 3/5

Value is less than 1.

Improper Fractions

Numerator is greater than or equal to denominator.

Example: 7/4

Value is 1 or greater.

Mixed Fractions

Combination of whole number and fraction.

Example: 2⅓

Represents whole quantities plus parts.

Equivalent Fractions

Different fractions representing the same value.

Example: 2/4 = 1/2

Created by multiplying/dividing numerator and denominator by the same number.

Unit Fractions

Numerator is always 1.

Example: 1/6

Building blocks for all fractions.

Complex Fractions

A fraction where the numerator, denominator, or both contain fractions.

Example: (1/2) / (3/4)

Can be simplified by multiplying the numerator by the reciprocal of the denominator.

Decimal Fractions

Fractions where the denominator is a power of 10.

Example: 0.75 is the decimal form of 3/4

These fractions are written as decimals rather than traditional fraction notation.

Converting Fractions

Improper to Mixed

  1. Divide numerator by denominator
  2. Quotient becomes the whole number
  3. Remainder becomes the new numerator
  4. Denominator stays the same

Example: 7/4 = 1¾

Mixed to Improper

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator
  2. Add the numerator to the result
  3. Keep the same denominator

Example: 2⅓ = 7/3

Fractions to Decimals

Divide the numerator by the denominator

Example: 3/4 = 0.75

Alternative: Find an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 10, 100, etc.

Fractions to Percentages

Multiply the fraction by 100

Example: 1/2 × 100 = 50%

Convert to decimal first if needed

Decimal to Fraction

  1. Write the decimal as a fraction with 1 in the denominator
  2. Multiply both the numerator and denominator by 10, 100, etc., to eliminate the decimal
  3. Simplify the fraction if needed

Example: 0.75 = 3/4

Fraction to Ratio

Write the fraction as a ratio of the numerator to the denominator

Example: 3/4 = 3:4

Operations with Fractions

Addition

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert fractions to equivalent forms with the LCD
  3. Add the numerators
  4. Keep the denominator the same
  5. Simplify the result if possible

Example: 1/4 + 1/2 = 1/4 + 2/4 = 3/4

Subtraction

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert fractions to equivalent forms with the LCD
  3. Subtract the numerators
  4. Keep the denominator the same
  5. Simplify the result if possible

Example: 3/4 - 1/2 = 3/4 - 2/4 = 1/4

Multiplication

  1. Multiply the numerators together
  2. Multiply the denominators together
  3. Simplify the resulting fraction

Example: 2/3 × 3/4 = 6/12 = 1/2

Division

  1. Flip the second fraction (find the reciprocal)
  2. Multiply the first fraction by the reciprocal
  3. Simplify the resulting fraction

Example: 2/3 ÷ 3/4 = 2/3 × 4/3 = 8/9

Adding Fractions with Unlike Denominators

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the LCD
  3. Add the numerators
  4. Keep the denominator the same
  5. Simplify the result if possible

Example: 1/3 + 1/4 = 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12

Subtracting Fractions with Unlike Denominators

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent fractions with the LCD
  3. Subtract the numerators
  4. Keep the denominator the same
  5. Simplify the result if possible

Example: 5/6 - 1/2 = 5/6 - 3/6 = 2/6 = 1/3

Operations with Complex Fractions

  1. First, simplify the complex fraction by multiplying the numerator by the reciprocal of the denominator
  2. If needed, perform additional operations (addition, subtraction, etc.) after simplification

Example: (1/2) ÷ (3/4) = (1/2) × (4/3) = 4/6 = 2/3

Comparing Fractions

Common Denominator Method

  1. Find the least common denominator (LCD)
  2. Convert both fractions to equivalent forms with the LCD
  3. Compare the numerators

Example: 3/5 vs 2/3 → 9/15 vs 10/15 → 2/3 is larger

Decimal Conversion

  1. Convert both fractions to decimal form
  2. Compare the decimal values

Example: 3/5 = 0.6 vs 2/3 ≈ 0.666 → 2/3 is larger

Cross-Multiplication

  1. Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second fraction
  2. Multiply the denominator of the first fraction by the numerator of the second fraction
  3. Compare the results

Example: 3/5 vs 2/3 → (3×3=9) vs (5×2=10) → 2/3 is larger

Comparing Mixed Fractions

  1. Convert mixed fractions to improper fractions
  2. Use any of the above methods (LCD, Decimal Conversion, Cross-Multiplication)

Example: 2⅓ vs 2½ → Convert to improper fractions: 7/3 vs 5/2 → Compare: 7/3 vs 5/2 → 7/3 is larger

Comparing Fractions with Negative Values

  1. Consider the sign of each fraction
  2. Follow any of the above methods to compare the absolute values
  3. Negative fractions will be smaller than positive fractions

Example: -3/4 vs 2/3 → -3/4 is smaller than 2/3

Real-World Applications

Cooking

  • Measuring ingredients in recipes
  • Adjusting recipe quantities
  • Dividing portions equally

Example: Doubling a recipe that calls for ¾ cup of flour (2 × ¾ = 1½ cups)

Finance

  • Calculating discounts and sales
  • Determining interest rates
  • Analyzing financial ratios

Example: Calculating 25% off a $120 item (25/100 × 120 = $30 off, new price = $90)

Measurements

  • Using inches and feet (imperial system)
  • Measuring cups and spoons
  • Construction and carpentry

Example: Cutting a board to 5⅜ inches (5 + ⅜ inches)

Science

  • Expressing chemical ratios
  • Dilution calculations
  • Probability and statistics

Example: Mixing a 1:4 ratio of chemical solution (1 part chemical to 4 parts solvent)

Time Management

  • Scheduling activities
  • Dividing work hours
  • Understanding proportions of time used

Example: If a task takes ¾ of an hour, you can calculate the time left by subtracting it from 1 hour (1 - ¾ = ¼ hour left)
