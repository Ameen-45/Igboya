Introduction to Word Problems

Word problems are mathematical scenarios presented in everyday language that require translation into mathematical expressions and equations for solving.

Why Word Problems Matter

  • Bridge between abstract math and real-world applications
  • Develop critical thinking and problem-solving skills
  • Essential for standardized tests and practical decision-making

Problem-Solving Mindset

Understand

Comprehend the scenario and what's being asked

Plan

Determine the approach and needed operations

Solve

Execute the mathematical operations

Verify

Check if the solution makes sense

Types of Word Problems

Arithmetic

  • Basic operations (+, -, ×, ÷)
  • Money calculations
  • Simple counting problems

Example: If 5 apples cost $2, what's the cost of 15 apples?

Algebraic

  • Requires equation formation
  • Variables represent unknowns
  • Age, number, and work problems

Example: John is twice as old as Sam. In 5 years, their ages will total 40. Find current ages.

Geometric

  • Shapes and measurements
  • Area, perimeter, volume
  • Spatial reasoning

Example: A rectangle's length is 3 more than its width. If perimeter is 26cm, find dimensions.

Ratio/Proportion

  • Comparative relationships
  • Scaling quantities
  • Mixture problems

Example: The ratio of boys to girls is 3:5. If there are 24 girls, how many boys?

Problem-Solving Strategies

Step-by-Step Approach

  1. Read Carefully

    Identify key information and what's being asked

  2. Define Variables

    Assign letters to unknown quantities

  3. Visualize

    Draw diagrams or make tables when helpful

  4. Form Equations

    Translate words into mathematical relationships

  5. Solve

    Perform the necessary calculations

  6. Verify

    Check if answer makes sense in context

Helpful Techniques

Underline Key Info

Highlight numbers and relationships

Eliminate Extras

Ignore irrelevant details

Work Backwards

Start from what's being asked

Estimate First

Predict reasonable answer range

Arithmetic Word Problems

Money Problem

Sarah bought 3 books at $12 each and 2 pens at $5 each. How much did she spend?

Solution:

Books: 3 × $12 = $36

Pens: 2 × $5 = $10

Total: $36 + $10 = $46

Time Problem

A train leaves at 3:15 PM and arrives at 7:45 PM. What's the travel duration?

Solution:

From 3:15 PM to 7:15 PM = 4 hours

Plus 30 minutes = 4 hours 30 minutes

Measurement Problem

A 5.2m rope is cut into 8 equal pieces. How long is each piece?

Solution:

5.2m ÷ 8 = 0.65m or 65cm per piece

Algebraic Word Problems

Age Problem

Mary is twice as old as John. In 5 years, the sum of their ages will be 40. Find current ages.

Solution:

Let John's age = x

Mary's age = 2x

In 5 years: (x+5) + (2x+5) = 40

3x + 10 = 40 → 3x = 30 → x = 10

John is 10, Mary is 20

Number Problem

The sum of three consecutive even integers is 78. Find the numbers.

Solution:

Let numbers be x, x+2, x+4

x + (x+2) + (x+4) = 78

3x + 6 = 78 → 3x = 72 → x = 24

Numbers are 24, 26, 28

Work Problem

Pipe A fills a tank in 3 hours, Pipe B in 5 hours. How long if both work together?

Solution:

A's rate: 1/3 tank/hour

B's rate: 1/5 tank/hour

Combined rate: 1/3 + 1/5 = 8/15

Time = 1 ÷ (8/15) = 15/8 = 1.875 hours

Geometry Word Problems

Rectangle Problem

A rectangle's length is 3cm more than its width. If perimeter is 26cm, find dimensions.

Solution:

Let width = w, length = w + 3

Perimeter: 2(w + w+3) = 26

2(2w + 3) = 26 → 4w + 6 = 26

4w = 20 → w = 5

Width = 5cm, Length = 8cm

Circle Problem

A circular garden has circumference of 31.4m. Find its radius (use π=3.14).

Solution:

C = 2πr → 31.4 = 2×3.14×r

31.4 = 6.28r → r = 5m

Volume Problem

A rectangular tank is 2m long, 1.5m wide, and holds 9m³ water. What's its height?

Solution:

Volume = l×w×h → 9 = 2×1.5×h

9 = 3h → h = 3m

Ratio & Proportion Problems

Basic Ratio

The ratio of boys to girls is 4:5. If there are 36 girls, how many boys?

Solution:

Boys/Girls = 4/5 = x/36

Cross-multiply: 5x = 144 → x = 28.8

Since count must be whole, likely 29 boys

Mixture Problem

How many liters of 20% solution must be added to 10L of 50% solution to get 30% solution?

Solution:

Let x = liters of 20% solution

0.20x + 0.50(10) = 0.30(x+10)

0.2x + 5 = 0.3x + 3 → 2 = 0.1x → x = 20L

Scale Problem

A model car is 1:24 scale. If model is 8cm long, how long is the real car?

Solution:

1/24 = 8/x → x = 8×24 = 192cm

Common Mistakes

Misreading

Skipping key words like "not", "except", or misinterpreting relationships

Example: Confusing "5 less than x" as x-5 vs 5-x

Unit Errors

Mixing different units (meters vs centimeters) without conversion

Example: Adding 2m + 30cm without converting to same unit

Operation Errors

Choosing wrong operation (adding instead of multiplying)

Example: Calculating total cost of 6 items at $3 each as 6+3 instead of 6×3

Verification

Not checking if answer makes logical sense

Example: Getting negative age or impossible measurements

Prevention Strategies

  • Read problems at least twice before solving
  • Underline or highlight key numbers and relationships
  • Draw diagrams for visual problems
  • Estimate reasonable answer range before calculating
  • Include units in all steps

Practical Example: Road Trip

Calculate total trip time and cost:

  • Distance: 450 miles
  • Average speed: 60 mph
  • Car efficiency: 30 mpg
  • Gas price: $3.50/gallon
  • Stops: 3 × 20 minutes each

Calculations:

Driving time: 450 ÷ 60 = 7.5 hours

Stop time: 3 × 20 = 60 minutes = 1 hour

Total time: 8.5 hours

Gas needed: 450 ÷ 30 = 15 gallons

Gas cost: 15 × $3.50 = $52.50



Practice Exercises

Arithmetic Problems

  1. A pizza is cut into 8 slices. If 3 people share it equally, how many slices each?
  2. You have $50 and spend $28.75. How much remains?
  3. If you buy 3 items priced at $10 each, how much do you spend in total?
  4. Divide $120 equally among 4 friends. How much does each friend get?
  5. A box contains 24 apples. If 6 apples are removed, how many are left?
  6. You have 15 candies. You give 5 to a friend. How many candies do you have left?
  7. If a book costs $12.50, how much would 4 books cost?
  8. If a car travels 60 miles per hour, how far will it travel in 4 hours?

Algebraic Problems

  1. One number is 5 more than another. Their sum is 23. Find both numbers.
  2. A father is 3 times as old as his son. In 10 years, he'll be twice as old. Find current ages.
  3. 3x + 7 = 16. Solve for x.
  4. 2x - 5 = 9. Solve for x.
  5. The sum of two numbers is 40. One number is 4 more than the other. Find both numbers.
  6. 5x - 8 = 12. Solve for x.
  7. If 3y + 5 = 20, what is the value of y?
  8. If x/2 + 6 = 18, what is the value of x?

Geometry Problems

  1. A triangle has sides 5cm, 12cm, and 13cm. What is its area?
  2. A circle's area is 78.5cm². Find its radius (π=3.14).
  3. Find the area of a rectangle with a length of 8cm and a width of 5cm.
  4. A square has a side length of 6cm. What is its perimeter?
  5. The perimeter of a triangle is 24cm. If two sides are 8cm and 10cm, what is the third side?
  6. A cylinder has a radius of 3cm and a height of 10cm. What is its volume? (Use π=3.14)
  7. The area of a parallelogram is 40cm². If the base is 8cm, what is the height?
  8. A trapezoid has bases of 6cm and 10cm, and a height of 4cm. What is its area?

Ratio Problems

  1. The ratio of sugar to flour in a recipe is 1:4. If you use 2 cups sugar, how much flour?
  2. In a class, the girl:boy ratio is 5:3. If there are 12 boys, how many girls?
  3. A recipe calls for a 3:2 ratio of butter to sugar. If you use 6 tablespoons of butter, how much sugar do you need?
  4. The ratio of students to teachers is 12:1. If there are 72 students, how many teachers are there?
  5. If the ratio of red to blue marbles is 3:5, and there are 15 red marbles, how many blue marbles are there?
  6. The ratio of cats to dogs in a park is 7:9. If there are 63 cats, how many dogs are there?
  7. A car's fuel efficiency is 8:1, meaning it uses 8 liters of fuel for every 1 liter of oil. If the car uses 40 liters of fuel, how much oil does it use?
  8. If a recipe requires a 2:3 ratio of flour to sugar, how much flour is needed if you are using 9 cups of sugar?
